Use z-score vs positional mean

⚠️ Refinement #1: The TD Dependency Scaling
Multiply by (tdPctBar / 30)

This assumes:

30% TD share is neutral baseline.

Is 30% actually the positional median?

For QBs it may be ~28–32%.
For RBs it could be closer to 35–40%.
For WRs it varies heavily by archetype.

If 30% is wrong, your penalty scaling gets biased.

Better:

Scale relative to positional median TD%.

Example:

tdDependencyFactor = playerTdPct / positionalMedianTdPct

That keeps it position-normalized.

⚠️ Refinement #2: Negative TD Boosts

You give a boost to below-average TD rates.

That’s mathematically consistent.

But in practice?

Below-average TD rate doesn’t guarantee positive regression. It often means:

Bad offense

Poor red zone role

Low aDOT

Weak QB

Boosting too much for low TD rate could inflate sustainability unrealistically.

I’d cap positive TD boost smaller than negative penalty.

Example:

Above average: full convex penalty

Below average: capped at +12 instead of +25

Regression upward is weaker than regression downward historically.

🟡 2️⃣ Efficiency Signal

Good structure. But one caution.

⚠️ Potential Double Counting for QBs

QB efficiency:

FP per weighted usage (z-score × 12)

TD rate already heavily influences FP per usage.

So TD rate and efficiency are partially correlated.

You are indirectly penalizing high TD QBs twice.

It may not break the model — but it amplifies TD effect.

Possible solution:
Remove TD component from FP per usage before computing efficiency.
Or accept that the TD penalty dominates and efficiency signal is secondary.

📊 What Is Sustainability?

Sustainability measures how likely a player’s current production level is to continue.

It evaluates:

• Touchdown rate vs league norms
• Efficiency per opportunity
• Weekly role stability
• Overall workload
• (For QBs) rushing floor

Players with extreme TD rates or unusually high efficiency face higher regression risk.

No formulas. No z-scores. No multipliers.
